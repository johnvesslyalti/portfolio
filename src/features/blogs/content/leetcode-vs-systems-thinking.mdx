I was grinding LeetCode.

## The question I couldn't stop sitting with

What happens when writing code becomes as cheap as having a thought?

Not in a sci-fi sense. In a very practical, already-happening sense.

AI can already generate APIs from a description. Scaffold projects in seconds. Debug with more context than most junior devs bring to the problem. Write tests, refactor existing code, explain unfamiliar codebases.

I've used these tools daily building RAG pipelines and agentic systems. You've probably used them too.

And when I stopped dismissing it as hype and actually sat with what it means — one question kept coming back:

> *If the cost of typing code is dropping fast, what am I actually making myself more valuable at?*

That question changed how I think about my time.

## The honest thing nobody says about LeetCode

![LeetCode Grind vs Systems Design](/blogs/leetcode-vs-systems-thinking/leetcode_vs_systems.png)

I want to be clear — I'm not here to tell you DSA is useless.

Algorithmic thinking matters. I've genuinely learned from it. Graph problems taught me things about reasoning I still use.

But there's an obsession in this industry where grinding 400+ problems becomes a substitute for engineering depth.

It becomes a ritual.

Solve mediums. Repeat. Get hired. Forget 80% of it in three months.

And the thing is — the skill LeetCode is really testing, pattern recognition on well-defined problems with clean inputs and expected outputs, is exactly the kind of thing AI is already good at.

That's not a knock on you. It's just an observation about where the bar is moving.

</Block>

## What actual engineering problems look like

![Real-world System Bottlenecks and Failures](/blogs/leetcode-vs-systems-thinking/system_failures.png)

Real problems don't come with constraints neatly written at the top.

They come with a Slack message at 11pm. A latency spike that only happens under a very specific combination of conditions. A query that runs fine in staging and falls apart with real data. A system that slows down when you add more workers instead of speeding up.

These aren't algorithm problems. They're systems problems.

And solving them requires a different kind of thinking — one that's much harder to develop by drilling isolated puzzles.

It requires understanding why things behave the way they do.

</Block>

## The shift I've been making

I've started spending more time on things that sit at the hard edge of automation:

- Distributed systems — not just the concepts, but the failure modes
- Database internals — not query syntax, but how execution actually works under load
- Caching and consistency — why adding a cache introduces a problem you didn't have before
- Queue architectures and backpressure — what happens when consumers can't keep up
- Observability — what it actually means to understand a system in production

Not because they're on a list somewhere.

Because when I'm debugging a RAG pipeline that behaves differently under concurrent load, or figuring out why a BullMQ worker pool is starving under specific conditions, these are the things that let me reason clearly about what's happening.

AI can generate the code for most of this.

It cannot yet tell you whether the design you're using creates a consistency problem three layers down in your specific system, with your specific guarantees, under your specific failure model.

That gap is where engineering judgment lives.

</Block>

## The amplification thing is real — but it cuts both ways

![AI-Amplified Systems Architect](/blogs/leetcode-vs-systems-thinking/ai_amplified_architect.png)

Here's the part that actually excites me.

AI doesn't just replace work. For engineers who understand what they're building, it amplifies output significantly.

I can move faster than I could two years ago. Prototype ideas in hours. Explore unfamiliar code without getting stuck on syntax. Iterate on designs without as much friction.

But the amplification only works if you understand what you're building.

An engineer who deeply understands how systems fail and has good AI tooling can do the work that used to take a team.

An engineer who only knows surface-level patterns with AI assistance just produces more surface-level work faster.

That gap is widening.

</Block>

## Where I think this is heading

I think software engineering is moving toward a version where:

- Smaller teams build significantly larger products
- The bottleneck shifts from "can we build this?" to "does this actually hold up?"
- Mechanical, repetitive implementation work shrinks
- System design, debugging, and architectural judgment become the differentiators

This isn't speculation. I've already seen it in how AI-first startups are building. One strong engineer with good tooling covering ground that would have required three people a few years ago.

Entry-level work that's purely implementation-heavy will feel the pressure first. Not vanish — but contract.

The engineers who come out of that shift well won't be the ones who memorized the most patterns.

They'll be the ones who can look at a failing system and know where to look.

Who can design something that stays coherent under growth.

Who understand why the tradeoff they made in week one becomes a problem in month six.

</Block>

## The actual takeaway

None of this means abandon LeetCode. Interviews exist. Fundamentals matter.

But if you're being honest with yourself about where your engineering time goes — it's worth asking how much of it is building skills AI is already commoditizing.

The future doesn't owe anyone relevance.

The engineers who stay valuable in an AI-saturated industry won't be the fastest coders.

They'll be the ones who think the deepest about what they're building and why.

That's the bet I'm making with my time.
